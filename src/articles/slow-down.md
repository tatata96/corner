# Slow Down Before You Code

## Working without a clear solution

Early in my career, starting a new feature felt almost paralyzing. Every approach seemed wrong before I'd even tried it. My instinct was to just open a file and start typing — waiting felt unproductive, like I wasn't really doing anything.

Over time, I've learned that slowing down before coding usually pays off. The discomfort of sitting with an unclear problem is worth pushing through. Better slow than spending two days building the wrong thing. I've done that before, usually because I didn't ask the right questions early enough.

## Clarify before you build

Most problems feel vague at first. A ticket, a rough requirement, a feature request — they rarely come with everything you need to know.

The difference now is that I don't treat that vagueness as a signal to start coding. I treat it as a signal to ask questions.

What is the actual goal here? What are the edge cases? What does success look like?

Writing these down, even informally, forces the problem to take shape. A few minutes of clear thinking can save hours of rework.

## Breaking it down

Once the problem is clearer, I focus on the smallest step that moves things forward. Not the full solution — just enough to make the next decision obvious.

A rough component structure, a quick sketch of the data flow, or even just defining what the inputs and outputs should be.

This also makes collaboration easier. A rough outline or a small prototype gives people something concrete to react to. It's much faster to align a team around something visible than something vague.

## Using tools to think, not just to build

More recently, tools like Claude Code have become part of how I clarify problems early. Not to generate a solution, but to think out loud — explore approaches, spot edge cases, or break something complex into smaller pieces.

It's like having a thinking partner available at the moment you need one most, before you've committed to anything.

The goal is still the same: understand the problem before building anything.

## Starting is still part of it

None of this means waiting until everything is perfect. At some point you have to build, and the first version is rarely the right one.

But there's a difference between starting with a rough plan and starting with no direction at all. The first leads somewhere. The second usually leads back to the beginning.

Slow down first. It saves more time than it costs.
